BLOOMBERG.  U.S. stocks tumbled to a three-month low, extending a global slump, as a record contraction in New York manufacturing spurred concern the governments stimulus package wont be enough to curb the deepening recession.  Bank of America Corp., Citigroup Inc. and JPMorgan Chase &amp; Co. lost at least 9 percent. Exxon Mobil Corp. was the biggest drag on the Standard &amp; Poors 500 Index as oil slid more than 7 percent. General Motors Corp., the biggest U.S. carmaker, lost as much as 18 percent before taking its case for more government support to the Treasury today. Banks led declines in Europe and Asia on concern they may face ratings downgrades and more losses.  The S&amp;P 500 retreated 3.8 percent to 795.06 at 1:48 p.m. in New York, dropping below 800 for the first time since November. The Dow Jones Industrial Average sank 253.12 points, or 3.2 percent, to 7,597.29. The Russell 2000 Index decreased 3.6 percent. U.S. markets were closed for Presidents Day yesterday, when shares in Asia and Europe fell.  Theres a growing sense that no matter whos in office, no matter how much they spend and on what, its going to be a long, painful process to get out of this situation and restore the economy to normalcy, said Michael Shinnick, manager of the $100 million Wasatch-1st Source Long/Short Fund, which has returned 1 percent this year as the S&amp;P 500 has retreated 11 percent.  All 10 industry groups in the S&amp;P 500 declined at least 1.7 percent after a Federal Reserve report showed manufacturing in New York contracted in February at the fastest pace on record, signaling the recession is deepening. The Fed Bank of New Yorks general economic index slid to minus 34.7, the lowest level since records began in 2001, from minus 22.2 percent in January.  Treasuries, Gold Gain  U.S. Treasury securities and gold advanced as investors sought protection against falling equity values. Newmont Mining Corp. led gold producers higher as the metal climbed 2.7 percent to $967.80 an ounce, the highest since July.  President Barack Obama today is expected to sign into law a $787 billion stimulus plan that combines tax breaks and spending designed to resuscitate the moribund U.S. economy.  U.S. stocks last week fell the most since November, with the S&amp;P 500 losing 4.8 percent, after Treasury Secretary Timothy Geithner failed to convince investors that the administrations bank rescue plan will work.  Stocks in Europe and Asia tumbled today after Moodys Investors Service said it might downgrade banks with units in Eastern Europe and the cost for South Korean banks to borrow dollars rose to a record. The two regions shares fell yesterday as Japans economy contracted the most since 1974, Britain was warned it faces the worst recession in almost three decades and the Group of Seven offered no solution to revive the global growth.  Big Shocker  The Japanese economy, the worlds second-biggest, contracted at an annual 12.7 percent pace in the fourth quarter, according to figures from the Cabinet Office. The U.K.s GDP probably will shrink 3.3 percent this year, instead of the 1.7 percent predicted in November, the Confederation of British Industry said yesterday.  The market is responding to Japans surprise drop in GDP, which was a big shocker over the weekend, Jack Ablin, chief investment officer at Harris Private Bank in Chicago, told Bloomberg Television.  Citigroup, the bank that received $45 billion from the government last year, lost 9.5 percent to $3.16. JPMorgan, the second-largest U.S. bank by assets, declined 9.2 percent to $22.43. Bank of America tumbled 9.2 percent to $5.01.  Exxon, GM Drop  Exxon Mobil, the worlds largest oil company, fell 4.1 percent to $71.54, the lowest since Nov. 21. Crude oil fell below $35 a barrel, extending its drop from a record $145.29 in July. Before December, oil hadnt closed below $35 a barrel since April 2004.  GM tumbled 15 percent to $2.12 for the biggest slide in the Dow average. The company and Chrysler LLC, already relying on government aid to survive, take their case to the U.S. Treasury today in an attempt to justify more U.S. f.  GM may seek support beyond an $18 billion request made Dec. 2 because of worsening economic conditions, people familiar with the companys plan said. The automaker is considering shutting or selling as many as four European plants as it tries to meet the terms of the government bailout, according to a person familiar with the proposals.  Put in a Base  The S&amp;P 500 fell as much as 4.5 percent to an intraday low of 789.91. The main benchmark for American equities tumbled more than 38 percent in 2008 for its worst year since 1937. The index had its lowest close of last year on Nov. 20 at 752.44, which was its lowest since April 1997. From that level it rebounded as much as 24 percent on Jan. 6.  Concern about the solvency of banks eroded the gains after Bank of America required a $138 billion federal bailout in order to complete its acquisition of Merrill Lynch &amp; Co. Financial shares fell more than 5 percent from Nov. 20 through last week, compared with gains of 4 percent to 20 percent for eight of the S&amp;P 500s nine other industry groups.  The market is making a real attempt to put in a base at these levels, said James Gaul, portfolio manager at Boston Advisors LLC, which manages $1.5 billion in Boston. If they dont hold, look out below. That would take us down to the 2002 lows, and there is not a lot of support from a technical standpoint for quite a ways down.  Wal-Mart Stores Inc. added $1.38, or 3 percent, to $47.91. The worlds largest retailer reported fourth-quarter profit excluding some items of $1.03 a share, beating the 99-cent average analyst estimate in a Bloomberg survey. Wal-Mart forecast first-quarter earnings of 72 cents to 77 cents a share, compared with an average analyst estimate of 77 cents.  Coca-Cola, Sirius XM  Coca-Cola Co., the worlds largest soft-drink maker, dropped 2.6 percent to $42.71. Coca-Cola Hellenic Bottling Co., the second-biggest bottler of Coke beverages, posted an unexpected fourth-quarter loss and said it was not meaningful to provide any earnings forecasts because of economic declines. Athens-based Coca-Cola HBC is 24 percent owned by Coca-Cola Co.  Sirius XM Radio Inc. jumped 71 percent to 18 cents. The satellite broadcaster will receive $530 million in loans from John Malones Liberty Media Corp. in exchange for board seats and an equity stake, avoiding a bankruptcy filing.  Wyndham Worldwide Corp. climbed 5.7 percent to $4.42. The largest seller of timeshare vacation units withdrew plans to sell as much as $200 million in new shares after its Feb. 13 announcement triggered a 30 percent drop in its existing shares.
