The Australian stock market closed sharply lower on Friday after the U.S. senate failed to agree on a rescue deal for the beleaguered U.S. auto industry. The benchmark S&amp;amp;P/ASX 200 index closed down 87.6 points or 2.4% at 3,510.4, extending its losses for the second straight trading sesssion. The All Ordinaries index shed 81.7 points or 2.3% to 3,452.5. For the week, the S&amp;amp;P/ASX 200 posted a 0.8% gain and the All Ordinaries managed a 0.7% rise.  In the currency market, the Australian dollar closed weaker against the greenback. The Aussie finished the domestic session at US$0.6568-0.6576, down from Thursday's close of US$0.6591-0.6595.  U.S. stocks fell sharply on Thursday amid fears that a bill to rescue the big-three automakers would not make it to the Senate and dismal economic and corporate news. The Dow Jones industrial average fell 196.3 points or 2.2% to 8,565.1, the broader Standard &amp;amp; Poor's 500 index fell 25.7 points or 2.9% to 873.6, and the Nasdaq composite index shed 57.6 points or 3.7% to 1,507.9.  Crude oil extended its losses in Asian trading on Friday after the U.S. Senate Majority Leader said that the legislature failed to reach an agreement on a bailout for the auto industry. At 00:08 a.m. ET, oil was quoted at $45.62 a barrel, down $2.36. The contract for January delivery soared 10% to settle at $47.98 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange on Thursday.  On the economic front, investors had little reports to digest on Friday.  In the resources sector, BHP Billiton plunged 4.6%, Rio Tinto slumped 9.5%, Fortescue Metals sank 11.0%, and Bluescope fell 4.4%. The gold miners were higher after the price of the metal fell in Sydney. Lihir Gold jumped 4.3% and Newcrest Mining advanced 2.9%. Energy sector leader Woodside Petroleum added 0.9%, but Santos fell 2.2% after the price of crude plunged on the back of U.S. car industry news.  Among banks, Westpac lost 3.1% and NAB dropped 1.3%, while ANZ advanced 1.0% and Commonwealth Bank closed unchanged.
