The Australian share market ignored a strong rally on Wall Street to close lower on Tuesday after heavy falls on local bank stocks.  At the 1620 AEDT close, the benchmark S&amp;amp;P/ASX200 index had lost 27.3 points, or 0.75 per cent, to 3,604.3, while the broader All Ordinaries was down 20.1 points, or 0.57 per cent, at 3,533.7.  On the Sydney Futures Exchange, the December share price index futures contract was down 28 points at 3,615 on a volume of 34,837 contracts, according to preliminary calculations. 'The market is a little bit disappointing following on from the (stronger) US leads from the metals markets overnight,' Man Financial broker Anthony Anderson said. 'Westpac threw the cat amongst the pigeons with its (capital) raisin g... so we saw weakness overall in the banking sector.' 'The real disappointment is following on from Rio last night - it had a massive move in London and hasn't really continued here. 'But I guess the spectre of capital raising is weighing on that stock so until we have some clarity there, we won't see any rallies of that stock.' Westpac said it would raise $2.5 billion from a placement of shares to institutional investors to strengthen its balance sheet.  The bank's shares are in a trading halt and last traded at $17.88.  National Australia Bank fell $1.06, or 5.06 per cent, to $19.90, Commonwealth Bank sank $2.80, or 8.54 per cent, to $30.00 and ANZ declined 83 cents, or 5.56 per cent, to $14.10.  In the resources sector, global miner BHP Billiton was up $1.23, or 4.52 per cent, to $28.45 and Rio Tinto added $1.27, or 3.96 per cent, to $33.35.  On Monday, the Dow Jones rose 298.76, or 3.46 per cent, to 8,934.18.  Litigation funds provider IMF Australia Ltd says it will fund claims by Oz Minerals Ltd shareholders relating to alleged breaches of its continuous disclosure obligations.  Oz Minerals shares were placed in a trading halt on November 28 and last traded at 55 cents.  While IMF was untraded at 77.5 cents.  Apparel manufacturer Pacific Brands Ltd lost 3.5 cents, or 5.98 per cent, to 55 cents after it cut its full year dividend by 65 per cent to help pay down debt and support its business in the soft economic environment.  Harvey Norman Holdings said written sales for the four weeks ended Sunday rose by 1.2 per cent compared to the same period in 2007, but retail margins remained under pressure.  Harvey Norman dropped seven cents, or 2.88 per cent, to $2.36.  Among other retailers, Wesfarmers shed 42 cents to $16.56, Woolworths was down 19 cents at $26.63 while David Jones picked up two cents to $2.89.  Woodchipper Gunns Ltd increased two cents to $1.05 after it said it expected its operating pre-tax profit for the first half of 2008/09 to exceed the result for the prior corresponding period.  At 1636 AEDT, the spot price of gold in Sydney was $US771.35 per fine ounce, up $US4.80 on Monday's close of $US766.55.  Gold stocks were mixed. Lihir was down two cents at $2.30, Newcrest was up $2.06, or 7.87 per cent, at $28.23 and Newmont lost nine cents to $4.56.  Oil prices rebounded from four-year lows in New York overnight to shoot above $US43 a barrel.  This came after OPEC floated the possibility of a 'severe' production cut and several countries announced new measures to boost their economies.  At close, Woodside Petroleum gave up 62 cents to $32.43, Santos dropped 35 cents to $12.90 and Oil Search gained two cents to $4.28.  As for media stocks, Fairfax says talks regarding the sale of its Southern Star television unit are continuing but no agreement has been concluded.  Its shares were up five cents, or 3.79 per cent, at $1.505 while News Corp shares were down nine cents at $13.16 and its non-voting scrip lost 10 cents to $12.40.  At 1650 AEDT, the most traded stock was Dexus Property Group, with 46.08 million shares worth $40.62 million changing hands.  Dexus shares were two cents higher at 90 cents.
