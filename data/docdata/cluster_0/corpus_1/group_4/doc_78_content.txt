Officials of Group of 20 nations met Saturday in Washington to discuss a common response to the financial and economic crises, but despite promising to better coordinate their actions to the stimulate their economies, there was little in their joint statement to suggest immediate intervention, and they put off until April any discussion of concrete measures to rebuild the global financial architecture.  Japan, meanwhile, reported Monday that its economy shrank at an annual pace of 0.4 percent in the third quarter after declining 3.7 percent in the second quarter, confirming expectations that the country had entered is first recession since 2001. 'The G-20 was pretty big on principles and pretty short on specifics,' Henk Potts, a fund manager at Barclays Stockbrokers in London, said. 'Economic data continue to be pretty poor and to weigh on investors,' he said, adding that the combination of contracting economic growth, falling corporate profits and rising unemployment suggested things could get worse. 'You only have to look at U.S. retail sales numbers to know that consumers are feeling nervous,' Potts said. The U.S. Commerce Department reported Friday that retail sales fell 2.8 percent in October from September, the worst such decline on record.  In midday trading, the DJ Euro Stoxx 50 index, a barometer of euro zone blue chips, fell 2.1 percent, while the FTSE 100 index in London fell 1.5 percent. The CAC 40 in Paris fell 1.6 percent, and the DAX in Frankfurt fell 1.5 percent.  Volkswagen fell 8.4 percent. The German automaker reported a 5.1 percent decline in October sales.  Financial institutions fell, with BNP Paribas declining 5.6 percent, HSBC Holdings falling 2.6 percent, and UBS declining 3.5 percent.  Trading in U.S. index futures suggested Wall Street stocks would open slightly lower.  Stocks were mixed in Asia. The Nikkei 225 stock average rose 0.7 percent, the Hang Seng index in Hong Kong slipped 0.1 percent, and the S&amp;amp;P/ASX 200 index in Sydney fell 2.5 percent.  U.S. crude oil futures for December delivery fell $1.16 to $55.88 a barrel.  The dollar fell against other major currencies.
