 Some analysts said after a string of recent declines, investors were increasingly being attracted by more appealing valuations. 'We see some bargain hunting emerging at the day's lows and the daily turnover is also increasing. Some investors are still selling to cut losses or whatever, but we see buyers there. The support is gradually emerging' said Y.K. Chan, strategist at Phillip Capital Management in Hong Kong. Japan's Nikkei 225 Average, which slid in the previous 10 sessions for its longest losing streak since 1965, finished 0.2% lower at 13,265.40. In 1953 the Nikkei fell for 12 straight sessions. The benchmark average crossed into positive territory a few times in the afternoon on Thursday, hitting a high of 13,326.95, but couldn't hold on to the gains. India's Sensitive Index, or Sensex dropped 3.2% at 13,230.14 by late-afternoon. Earlier if fell as much as 5%, giving up nearly all the gains it booked Wednesday when traders covered their short sales, or bets that the market would fall further. In Hong Kong, the benchmark Hang Seng Index fell 2.1% to 21,242.78 and the Hang Seng China Enterprises Index lost 4% to 11,139.92. The Shanghai Composite rose 2% to 2,703.53 and the Shenzhen All Share index soared 3.5% to 811.76, with both indexes coming off initial declines. In Taipei, the Weighted index gained 0.6% to 7,394.10. In Seoul, the Kospi fell 1.1% to 1,606.54, while Australia's S&amp;amp;P/ASX 200 shed 1.9% to 4,998.30, dropping below the 5,000-point mark for the first time since September 2006. Elsewhere, New Zealand's NZX 50 index fell 2.2% to 3,094.42 and Singapore's Straits Times fell 1.4% to 2,868.03 by late afternoon. The latest bout of selling came after crude oil prices hit a record high during the session and U.S. stocks fell sharply overnight, amid concerns about the health of its economy. In a note to clients, Citigroup global strategist Michael W. Joynson wrote that global stock prices fell 12% in the first half of 2008, representing 'one of the weakest starts to the year since 1970.' 'Despite the plethora of bad news, we resist the obvious temptation to turn wholesale sellers of global equities. Much of the bad news seems to be in the price,' Joynson wrote. 'The most recent sell-off has made a cheap asset class even cheaper. On some measures, equity valuations are back down to levels we last saw in the 1980s.' August oil futures soared to a record high of $145.09 a barrel in electronic trading, before slipping back to $144.88 a barrel recently for a gain of $1.31. The contract closed at a record high of $143.57 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange Wednesday, adding $2.60.
