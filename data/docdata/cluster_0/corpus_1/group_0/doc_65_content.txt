 The Institute for Supply Management reported its purchasing managers' index came in at 50.2 in June, showing modest growth, a modest change from a contraction-indicating 49.6 in May.Though encouraging, the report was not without its disappointments. The employment portion of the index contracted faster in June, prices increased more rapidly, and inventories remained to high while new orders slowed.Still, the data jazzed up investors, as Wall Street trimmed its losses and even popped into positive territory briefly.Just before midday the Dow was down 38 points, or 0.3%, at 11,312, the S&amp;amp;P 500 fell 4 points, or 0.3%, to 1,276, and the Nasdaq was off 7 points, or 0.3%, to 2,286.Meanwhile, oil prices were about a dollar below session highs, as crude was up $2.50 at $142.50 a barrel.Treasuries also reversed course on the ISM report, as the 10-year note was yielding 3.98%, up from 3.89% earlier in the day, and just below Monday's close of 3.99%.Tuesday's next inflection points come in afternoon trading, when domestic auto sales figures from June are due.General Motors (nyse: GM - news - people ) and Ford Motor (nyse: F - news - people ) were expected to report sharp declines, 21.5% and 18.5% respectively on an adjusted basis, as weaker consumer demand takes a bite out of sales.The automakers have taken a variety of steps to counteract falling demand, including shifting focus toward more fuel-efficient vehicles and announcing other restructuring initiatives, such as Ford's delaying a planned update of its popular pickup truck, the F-150.Shares of Ford were down 25 cents, or 5.2%, to $4.56 Tuesday morning, before June sales fell 28.1% from 2007.GM lost 54 cents, or 4.7%, to $10.96, with investors still awaiting its results.Lehman Brothers (nyse: LEH - news - people ) was on the mend, after shares were battered Monday thanks to a rumor the firm could sell itself to Barclays (nyse: BCS - news - people ) at a steep discount.
