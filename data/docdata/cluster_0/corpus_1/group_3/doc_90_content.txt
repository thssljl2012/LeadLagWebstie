HONG KONG (Dow Jones) -- Asian markets declined Friday after fears of a recession sent Wall Street stocks sharply lower, with machinery stocks like Komatsu suffering heavy losses in Tokyo. A retreat in commodity prices hurt BHP Billiton in Sydney and Cnooc in Hong Kong.'The hedge funds have been liquidating the leading stocks to get the liquidity for themselves. They are getting redemption requests probably every day. That's bearing very badly on leading stocks,' said Stuart Smith, private client adviser at Bell Potter Securities.In Tokyo, the Nikkei 225 Average fell as low as 10,943.93 during the morning session, dropping below the 11,000-point level for the first time since May 2005. The benchmark was recently down 1.5% at 10,982.53, while the broader Topix index fell 2.5% to 1,050.50.Australia's S&amp;amp;P/ASX 200 index dropped 2.2% to 4,657.90, New Zealand's NZX 50 index shed 2.5% to 3,151.54, Taiwan's Taiex gave up 0.6% to 5,670.92, Singapore's Straits Times index lost 2% to 2,315.32 and Thailand's SET index dipped 0.9% to 592.19.Smith said Sydney stocks, in particular, could extend losses Monday as well on weak sentiment, but were likely to bounce Tuesday as the Reserve Bank of Australia was expected by economists to reduce its benchmark interest rates by half-a-percentage-point.South Korean and mainland Chinese markets were closed for a holiday.In Mumbai, the Sensitive Index, or Sensex, dropped 1.4% to 12,871.68 in the early minutes.In Hong Kong, the Hang Seng Index fell 2.2% to 17,813.32, while the Hang Seng China Enterprises Index declined 2.7% to 9,078.89, with both indexes reversing gains from the previous session.Patrick Shum, strategist at Karl Thomson Securities, said investors were cautious ahead of the U.S. House of Representatives vote on the revised $700 billion financial-sector rescue package Friday, and that stocks could bounce Monday if they approve it.'The financial crisis isn't over yet, and the market direction will depend on whether it will worsen by the end of the year. I guess the market may rebound a little, but the trend is still looking down,' said Shum.Regional detailIn Tokyo, shares of machinery maker Komatsu (KMTUY) slumped 9.8% a day the after it lost nearly 11%, on persistent worries about slowing global demand.Exporters were also hit, with Nintendo Co. (NTDOY) shrinking 9.1% and Nikon Corp. (NINOY) losing 9.4%.Automobile companies extended losses after reporting weak U.S. sales earlier this week, with Toyota Motor Corp. (TM) sliding 6% and Honda Motor Co. (HMC) stumbling 4.7%.The drop in Toyota shares came after the automobile giant said it would offer no-interest loans on 11 vehicle models in the U.S., seeking to attract potential buyers sidelined by the credit crisis.The regionwide decline came after weak factory orders () and jobless claims data stirred recession fears in the U.S. overnight, weighing down stocks on Wall Street.In Hong Kong, Hang Seng Bank (HSNGY) dropped 5.6% a day after the Hong Kong lender said it held 'certain senior debt securities' issued by Washington Mutual, although it added that 'such exposure is immaterial.'Resource stocks were also weighed down by sharp losses in commodity prices overnight, with BHP Billiton (BHP) dropping 4.5% and Rio Tinto (RTP) shedding 4.7% in Sydney. In Hong Kong, Cnooc fell 4.5% and gold miner Zijin Mining Group Co. (601899.SH) shed 6.1%, while Inpex Holdings Inc. fell 6.4% in Tokyo.November crude-oil futures slipped 86 cents to $93.11 a barrel in electronic trading, after dropping $4.56 to $93.97 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange. Gold for December delivery slid $2.4 to $841.90 an ounce in electronic trading, after giving up $43 to end at $844.30 an ounce on the Nymex.In Asian currency trading, the U.S. dollar bought 105.23 yen, compared with 105.29 yen late Thursday.On Wall Street, the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJI) slumped 3.2% to 10, 482.85 and the Nasdaq Composite (RIXF) tumbled 4.5% to 1,976.72, while the S&amp;amp;P 500 index (SPX) plummeted 4% to 1,114.28.(END) Dow Jones Newswires 10-03-080229ET Copyright (c) 2008 Dow Jones &amp;amp; Company, Inc.
